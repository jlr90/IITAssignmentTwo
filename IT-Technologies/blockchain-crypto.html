    Blockchain and Cryptocurrency

    Source: https://www.blockchains-expert.com/en/introduction-to-blockchain/

    A blockchain, in its basic form, is a series of time-stamped records that cannot be retroactively altered. These records, named ‘blocks’ are decentralised, meaning that they are managed by a collection of computers that are not owned by any single entity (Rosic, 2016). Each block is secured and bound to each other, in way of a link to the previous block, and encrypted.

    In short, a “blockchain is a database that is distributed among many computers” (Casey, 2018) and any changes to the database “require the consensus of the other computers”.

    To ensure that nobody can alter the timestamp, which functions as somewhat of an internal audit trail, the blockchain makes use of a protection method called Proof of Work (PoW). Proof of Work is a method created to ensure that all digital transactions are secure without the need of a third party (Lindsey, 2018). This contrasts with Secure Sockets Layer (SSL) which requires a link between a server and user for verification (Vocell, 2020).

    The way in which these records can be verified as untampered is through using Public-key Cryptography, also called Asymmetric Cryptography. In contrast with symmetric encryption “where any arbitrary series of bytes of appropriate length can serve as a key” (Loganathan, 2017), Public-key Cryptography requires specially crafted key pairs. These crafted key pairs are referred to as the private key and public key. While the public key can be known to anybody, and shared with others, the private key should be known only by you. As a public key is generated from a private key, they “are relatively easy to calculate in one direction, and are practically impossible to calculate in the opposite direction” (Richard, 2019). Therefore, the owner of a private key can “confidently distribute their public key with the knowledge that no one will be able to reverse the function”.

    Blockchain technology was conceptualised and has been researched and in development for decades but did not come into prominence until the emergence of Bitcoin in 2008. This technology is widely considered as useful only for use in respect to cryptocurrency, however there are other clear and valuable uses. A use of this technology which is far removed from cryptocurrency is the example of its use in preventing voter fraud. Just as the timestamped blocks are used to verify transactions with cryptocurrency to prevent fraud, it can also be used to deliver an electronic platform for vote-counting that cannot be hacked (Satara, 2017).

    While there are uses beyond cryptocurrency, as outlined above, they are still not widely used, and the technology itself is still somewhat undefined. As stated by Satara, we “are in an exploration stage, far from any established or proven solutions”.

    To date, blockchain has made the biggest impact on the finance sector. That said, it is currently used in the supply chain, food safety and health care sectors, to name a few. These are all vital services where investment in this emerging technology is widely advantageous. The benefits of this technology are really apparent when looking at the health care and life sciences sector. The integration of blockchain technology with the supply chain of pharmaceutical companies ensures the integrity and traceability of the products being distributed and would prevent counterfeit drugs reaching end users (Singh, 2020). This prevention is, again, due to the inability to hack or change the prior records on the blockchain.

    Blockchain technology will likely have an impact on many aspects of our daily life, directly or indirectly and possibly without your knowledge. As Blockchain technology “allows peer to peer transactions as people can trust each other in the networks” (Griffin, 2017), the need for intermediaries, such as banks for the verification of digital transactions, become unnecessary. This does not mean, however, that banks are therefore obsolete. It can be argued that while Blockchain is an emerging and disruptive technology, it should be viewed as an advancement on current security protocols which offers a promise of transformation of both the internet as well as the global economy and our daily lives.

    Image: Comparison between centralised ledgers and distributed ledgers
    Source: https://tradeix.com/distributed-ledger-technology/

    The established concepts around Blockchain technology and cryptocurrencies is largely seen as beneficial in developed nations like the United States and Europe. For example, the Australian Stock Exchange is now in the process of transitioning from their current CHESS technology to their new distributed ledger technology which promises to “provide issuers and end investors with greater control over, and enhanced confidence in, their market activities through timely, secure and simplified access to the register of holders (for issuers), financial assets (end investors) and associated information” (ASX, 2020). However, there is a huge benefit for people in developing nations as well, where access to technology is far more limited, and “where the state does not provide a good trust model” (Castor, 2020). The issue faced here though, is that access to computers and Wi-Fi is limited, and while they do have access to 3G and smartphones, these devices are not currently able to handle downloading an entire blockchain. Therefore, alternative workarounds are required.

    The biggest impact likely to affect the day-to-day life of most individuals in the short-term future will be the use of Blockchain for funds transfers, both locally and internationally. Currently, our traditional financial services providers such as banks are “lagging behind the pace of technology development” (Sergeenkov, 2018). Through the elimination of the requirement to have a bank verify a transaction, the transfer of funds at point of sale could speed up payments at lower costs than what is currently charged by banks. Similarly, use of this technology could reduce operating costs and allow near on real-time transactions between vendors, consumers, and financial institutions.

    Further to bank settlements, Blockchain can be used for currency exchange to transfer funds internationally both cost effective, and in a timely manner. This is a huge advantage that Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies such as Ethereum and XRP offers. However, the “transition to Bitcoin payments among banks and other established financial institutions is likely to be a slow one” (Lui, 2017) due to the fact the technology is still mostly under development, but largely due to the delay in regulators and governments adopting digital currencies.
